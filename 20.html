It's one where people feel you can't just agree to disagree; you're either on their side or you're a bad person. How could you not support the only democracy in the Middle East and side with the antisemitic terrorists who rape innocents and use their own people as human shields? No, wait, how could you possibly support the fascist apartheid state that's trying to genocide Palestinians for their colonialist project? And no, sitting this one out and having no opinion isn't a safe bet either. I've watched this topic divide my friends. It gets weirdly heated and personal for something that has no effect on us happening so far from home. I've been speaking with more left-leaning people than right-leaning people these days, and some of them have taken issue with me for seeming too pro-Israel, though I think they have warped standards and in reality I'm just normal and moderate about it. I also feel the West should be making our support for Israel more conditional, making demands of them, drawing some red lines about what we're comfortable with them doing if they want to stay in our good graces. The slow expansion into the West Bank seems plainly unjustified to me, their occupation of the Golan Heights should probably end (less sure), and although it hasn't happened yet, I certainly wouldn't approve of Israel expanding into Gaza, should that happen in the future (that should probably be a red line drawn before it happens). In the worst light, Israel appears similar to Russia, as a country that is annexing territory from its neighbours simply because it can, and should be condemned similarly. However, anti-Zionist hard-liners have shown me they're often black-and-white thinkers (you must want to destroy Israel or you support a genocide of Arabs) who are extremely biased in their thinking (every Israel does is viewed through the most critical lens possible, while we have endless understanding and compassion for their enemies) who play fast and loose with the facts and language (everything Israel does is genocide, aparthheid, ethnic cleansing, fascism, etc.), which they're attached to and insistent on (in order to control the framing of the conversation, they often refuse to proceed to talking about the issue if you don't agree with all the labels they apply to it). For these reasons, I've often found myself in contentious conversations where I'm stuck playing the part of 'Israel defender' simply because the person who is condemning Israel is going so unreasonably about it. For the purpose of clarity in discourse, I've put together a list of claims that I would make or defend about the topic, some more anti-Israel and some more pro-Israel. ### **This conflict's importance is overstated** There are many conflicts happening across the world at any one time. For people who are living directly in the region where conflict is occurring, the outcome of said conflict is important, but what impact its resolution will have on the rest of the world varies. The conflict between Ethiopia and Somalia probably doesn't really matter to anybody who's not Ethiopian or Somalian, for instance. And who outside of the region involved cares about the outcome of the Guyana--Venezuela dispute? By contrast, some conflicts do have a broader global importance. The Russia--Ukraine war and any potential conflict between China and Taiwan are both examples of this. Regardless of where you're from, you should care about what precedent ends up being set for international politics by these issues. Israel--Palestine is not. Which side is going to end up winning, Israel or Hamas and Hezbollah? ### **Israel has a compelling, heroic narrative** Let's start here, with something that seems comparatively trivial.
